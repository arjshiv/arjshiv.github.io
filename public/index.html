Story

How I got here

My original plan was academia. Both my parents have PhDs, so that felt like the normal version of ambition. What pulled me out of that track was simple. I wrote some code in a lab, it saved real time, and the result was useful immediately. That is a hard feedback loop to unsee.

Still true

Coding is easy, engineering is hard.

I keep coming back to that line because it explains most of what I like. If you do not understand the context, the workflow, and the actual constraint, you are not doing engineering yet. A lot of my career has been finding places where the loop was slow, the context was missing, and the flashy answer was the wrong one.

Cornell

Code started as a lab shortcut.

I was working in an electron microscopy lab and part of the job was cleaning up magnetic noise in the room. It was slow, manual work. I realized an early iPhone already had the sensor I needed, so I wrote code to speed that up. That was the first time software felt real to me. You could take something hidden, make it visible, and save people time immediately. Once you feel that, it is hard to go back.

BlackRock

I learned to ask for what I wanted, and be nice about it.

I did not get the job the first time. I followed up because I liked the problems. They felt real. Six months later another role opened, I re-interviewed, and then asked if New York was possible instead of Delaware. That turned into a role building products that made institutional tools usable for advisors. It is still one of the more useful lessons of my career: ask for what you want and be nice about it.

Climb Credit

The question was simple: does this improve outcomes?

I led product, engineering, and data around underwriting tied to earnings outcomes. The market default was to look at the applicant as they were today. The more useful question was what happens to earnings after the program. Once you set that bar, many other decisions get easier. The work helped grow annual loan volume from $1 million to $300 million.

ResiDesk

Housing does not have a survey problem. It has a signal problem.

Renting is something half the planet does, and we all know how much it sucks when life at your apartment sucks. Residents are already telling operators what matters. The information shows up in fragments. They talk when the sink breaks, when the Wi-Fi fails, when the leasing office does not respond, when they are deciding whether to stay. We use those conversations to show what affects renewals, rent, and NOI before the market pays for the mistake. The point is not replacing the human relationship. The point is making the relationship informed.

How I try to be useful outside my own company

The bar

If you are going to help, show up with context and better questions than the machine would have asked.

I have invested in more than 100 startups and mentored through Techstars. I like founders who are unusually serious about a real problem. Not trend chasers. Not tourists. People who know the market in detail, stay close to the customer, and keep asking what is true even when the easier move is to perform certainty.

The reason I still like this work in the age of LLMs is that the job changed. Advice used to be periodic commentary. Now the floor is much higher. Good help makes the next decision clearer. It does not show up once a month with vague memory and a strong tone.

Writing

What I keep coming back to

The same few ideas show up in the products, the talks, and the essays. The loop is usually slower than people admit. The bottleneck is often conversational before it is technical. And the demo is rarely the business.

Start with the actual workflow.

If you do not know what the user is trying to get done, you are decorating the surface. Many product mistakes are context mistakes first.

Do not confuse the demo with the product.

A flashy demo is easy. A tool people use every day in a messy workflow is hard. That is usually where the value is.

Use AI to improve judgment, not replace it.

There is real value between zero and full automation. If the system gets you 80 percent of the way there, a person can spend judgment on the last 20 percent.

Decompose until the eval is obvious.

Most fuzzy AI debates come from bad problem definition. Break the task down far enough and the success condition usually gets clearer.
