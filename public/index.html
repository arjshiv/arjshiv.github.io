Story

The path only looks random

My original plan was academia. Both my parents have PhDs, so that felt like the respectable route. Then I wrote some code in a lab and watched it save real time for real people. That is a difficult experience to unlearn.

Still true

Coding is easy, engineering is hard.

I still come back to that line because it explains most of what I like. If you strip out the workflow, the incentives, and the constraint, you usually end up with a demo. Most of my career has been about the opposite.

Cornell

Code started as a lab shortcut.

I was working in an electron microscopy lab and part of the job was cleaning up magnetic noise in the room. It was slow, manual work. I realized an early iPhone already had the sensor I needed, so I wrote code to speed that up. That was the first time software felt real to me. You could take something hidden, make it visible, and save people time immediately. Once you feel that, it is hard to go back.

BlackRock

I did not get the job. Then I got the right job.

I did not get the job the first time. I followed up because I liked the problems. They felt real. Six months later another role opened, I re-interviewed, and then asked if New York was possible instead of Delaware. That turned into a role building products that made institutional tools usable for advisors. It is still one of the better lessons of my career: ask for what you want and be nice about it.

Climb Credit

The interesting part was asking a better question.

I led product, engineering, and data around underwriting tied to earnings outcomes. The market default was to look at the applicant as they were today. The better question was what happens to earnings after the program. Once you set that bar, many other decisions get easier. The work helped grow annual loan volume from $1 million to $300 million.

ResiDesk

Housing does not have a survey problem. It has a signal problem.

Renting is something half the planet does, and we all know how much it sucks when life at your apartment sucks. Residents are already telling operators what matters. The information shows up in fragments. They talk when the sink breaks, when the Wi-Fi fails, when the leasing office does not respond, when they are deciding whether to stay. We use those conversations to show what affects renewals, rent, and NOI before the market pays for the mistake. The point is not replacing the human relationship. The point is making the relationship informed.

Work

What changed

I care less about whether a product looks smart and more about whether it changes behavior. Does the right information get to the right person in time? Does a better decision become easier to make? That is the bar.

ResiDesk

7%

Higher renewals and rent after getting closer to what residents were saying, and acting on it before it turned into churn.

Climb Credit

$1M → $300M

Annual loan growth tied to underwriting around outcomes, not generic credit stories or abstract risk language.

BlackRock

$40M ARR

Retail analytics product from zero to $40 million in ARR in year one by making institutional infrastructure usable for advisors.

ResiDesk

Co-founder

Property management is full of information hiding in plain sight. Renewal risk, payment stress, maintenance pain, staff responsiveness. Most of it dies in disconnected systems and half-finished conversations. ResiDesk turns that mess into something operators can act on. The chatbot is the wrapper. The decision is the product.

Climb Credit

CTO and CPO

The market wanted to underwrite the person as they were. We cared about what happened after the program. That one change resets everything: who gets funded, what data matters, how fast you can say yes, and what a good outcome looks like. It kept product, underwriting, and data tied to something real.

BlackRock

Product and engineering lead

The interesting part was not the data. It was the translation. Institutional infrastructure is only valuable if an advisor can actually use it. Same information. Better interface. Better business. That lesson never really left.

Investing

What I do when it is not my company

The bar

If you are going to help, show up with context and better questions than the machine would have asked.

I have invested in more than 100 startups and mentored through Techstars. I like founders who are unusually serious about a real problem. Not tourists. Not trend chasers. People who know the market in detail, stay close to the customer, and keep asking what is true even when the easier move is to perform certainty.

The reason I still like this work in the age of LLMs is that the bar went up. Advice used to be periodic commentary. Now good help makes the next decision clearer. If it sounds good but does not change the next move, it is mostly decoration.

Writing

Ideas I keep returning to

Same obsessions, different surface area. The loop is usually slower than people admit. The bottleneck is often conversational before it is technical. And the demo is rarely the business.

Most product mistakes are context mistakes.

If you do not know what the user is trying to get done, you are decorating the surface.

A demo is not a product.

A flashy demo is easy. A tool people use every day in a messy workflow is hard. That is usually where the value is.

Most of the value sits before full automation.

If the system gets you 80 percent of the way there, a person can spend judgment on the last 20 percent.

Break the problem down until the eval is boring.

Most fuzzy AI debates come from bad problem definition. Break the task down far enough and the success condition usually gets obvious.
