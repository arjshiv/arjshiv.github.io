Story

How I got here

My original plan was a PhD. Both my parents have one, and that was the world I understood. What changed was seeing how fast software could turn an idea into something useful.

A line I keep coming back to

Coding is easy, engineering is hard.

That line is not about difficulty for its own sake. It is about context. The hard part is knowing what problem matters, where the signal is buried, and what people will trust enough to use.

Cornell

Code started as a lab shortcut.

In an electron microscopy lab, I used an early iPhone magnetometer to detect magnetic noise in the room and cut setup time. That was the first time I felt what software could do in a real setting.

BlackRock

I learned to ask for what I wanted.

I did not get the job the first time. I followed up, re-interviewed months later, and asked if New York was possible instead of Delaware. That turned into a role building products that made institutional tools usable for advisors.

Climb Credit

The question was simple: does this improve outcomes?

I led product, engineering, and data around underwriting tied to earnings outcomes. The work helped grow annual loan volume from $1 million to $300 million.

ResiDesk

Housing does not have a survey problem. It has a signal problem.

Residents tell operators what matters every day, but the information shows up in fragments. We use those conversations to show what affects renewals, rent, and NOI before the market pays for the mistake.

Investing

Where I spend time and money

What I look for

Real market pain. Strong taste. Fast learning loops. Founders who know the problem cold.

I have invested in more than 100 startups and mentored through Techstars. The filter is simple. I want founders who are close to the customer, allergic to fake signal, and willing to keep asking what is true.

The same logic shows up in advising. Good advice is a system, not a personality. It should make the next decision clearer, not make the room feel smarter.

Thinking

How I reason about building

Start with context.

If you do not know what problem you are solving, you are probably not going to solve it.

Do not chase the demo.

Pay attention to the part people are not demoing. That is usually where the real operating problem lives.

Use AI to sharpen human judgment.

The model should get you 80 percent of the way there so people can spend judgment on the last 20 percent.

Decompose the problem.

Most hard work gets easier once you break it into pieces small enough to test against reality.
