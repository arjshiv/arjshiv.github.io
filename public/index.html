There is usually signal in the system already. I build the layer that makes it usable.

The logos change. The problem does not. I saw it in a Cornell microscopy lab, then in finance at BlackRock, then in education finance at Climb Credit, and now in housing at ResiDesk. Important information is already in the system. It shows up too late, in fragments, or inside tools built for the wrong person. Story

How I got here

My original plan was academia. Both my parents have PhDs, so that felt like the natural path. What changed was writing code for the first time and realizing you could think of something in the morning and make it useful by the afternoon. That was a different feeling from studying something that might matter years later.

A line I keep coming back to

Coding is easy, engineering is hard.

That line is not about showing off how hard the work is. It is about context. If you do not know what problem you are solving, you are probably not going to solve it. A surprising amount of my career has been finding places where the loop was slow, the context was missing, and the obvious answer was wrong.

Cornell

Code started as a lab shortcut.

I was working in an electron microscopy lab and part of the job was cleaning up magnetic noise in the room. It was slow, manual work. I realized an early iPhone already had the sensor I needed, so I wrote code to speed that up. That was the first time software felt real to me. You could take something hidden, make it visible, and save people time immediately.

BlackRock

I learned to ask for what I wanted, and be nice about it.

I did not get the job the first time. I followed up, stayed in touch, re-interviewed months later, and asked if New York was possible instead of Delaware. That turned into a role building products that made institutional tools usable for advisors. The useful lesson was not persistence for its own sake. It was that context matters, and people respond better when you are direct about what you want.

Climb Credit

The question was simple: does this improve outcomes?

I led product, engineering, and data around underwriting tied to earnings outcomes. To me, that was the right question. Not does this look clever, but does this program change what someone earns after they finish? Once you anchor on that, many other decisions get easier. The work helped grow annual loan volume from $1 million to $300 million.

ResiDesk

Housing does not have a survey problem. It has a signal problem.

Renting is something half the planet does, and we all know how much it sucks when life at your apartment sucks. Residents are already telling operators what matters. The information shows up in fragments. They talk when the sink breaks, when the Wi-Fi fails, when the leasing office does not respond, when they are deciding whether to stay. We use those conversations to show what affects renewals, rent, and NOI before the market pays for the mistake.

Investing

Where I spend time and money

What I look for

Real market pain. Strong taste. Fast learning loops. Founders who know the problem cold.

I have invested in more than 100 startups and mentored through Techstars. The filter is simple. I like founders who are close to the customer, who understand the market in detail, and who are willing to keep asking what is true instead of hiding behind momentum.

The same logic shows up in advising. Good advice should make the next decision clearer. It should not be low-frequency commentary from someone parachuting in for an hour. The new bar is higher. Context is cheap now. If you are going to help, you need to show up with more context than the obvious answer.

Thinking

How I reason about building

Most of my views reduce to the same few ideas. The loop is usually slower than people admit. The bottleneck is often conversational before it is technical. And the highest value move is usually getting closer to what the customer is already telling you.

Start with context.

If you do not know what problem you are solving, you are probably not going to solve it.

Do not chase the demo.

Pay attention to the part people are not demoing. That is usually where the real operating problem lives. The flashy layer is rarely the hard part.

Use AI to sharpen human judgment.

It is not a zero or one hundred percent game. If the model gets you 80 percent of the way there, people can spend judgment on the last 20 percent. That is where the value usually is.

Decompose the problem.

Decompose, decompose, decompose until the question is small enough to test against reality. Most fuzzy arguments get clearer once you force them down to the useful part.
